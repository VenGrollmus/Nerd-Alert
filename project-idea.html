Project Idea

Overview

This project will design a tool that can empower staff by using software with conversational interactions using a chatbot. A chatbot could be a valuable tool to support staff within HR, Finance, and Staff Resources on services offered, process information, legislation, and policy details offered at the organisation. The chatbot will take input in a conversational manner, process the query and find an answer to match the request, then respond. On the outside the transcript will look like a normal conversation with two parties responding to each other.

The chatbot's function would act as a training tool by receiving input from an individual, processing the input using NLP (Natural Language Processing), and provide an answer (What makes a great chatbot? Laser focus on customers, 2022). The input would be in the form of questions related to matters we usually get from customers and colleagues, pointing them in the right direction or giving them resources that could help them find the answers.  The chatbot will be trained to recognise topics and keywords and choose the most appropriate and relevant response using the NLP Nltk module. This tool would make life easier for individuals in positions such as frontline customer support in large and complex organisations.

Motivation

Chatbots are familiar with large companies that use the technology in online sales or provide basic customer service; I have had many experiences with speaking to a chatbot on my bank app regarding my account or finding a product on a website to buy. In my experience, there does not appear to be much of this technology used in the workplace to support internal staff. The focus and demand are more on how customer experience can be improved within a business and therefore improve the organisation's image.

The project motivation cornerstone is based on helping people and I want to drive the focus of the plan towards supporting staff to do their jobs in an efficient and timely manner by creating this tool, building up a knowledge base, and then teaching it to provide the most appropriate answers. The points of conversational user experience outlined by Microsoft (Conversational User Experience Guide, 2021) will allow us to create a solid plan on how we want the chatbot to respond, give it a personality, and map out typical conversations from the user perspective. That will give us a good base to set up the appropriate environment and data sets to train the chatbot.

The Chatbots' classification below shows that a chatbot can be developed and have multiple categories and uses. The categories usually begin from one area and is then expanded over time as required. The algorithms and techniques employed in the chatbot design determines if the chatbot is rule-based or machine learning (Adamopoulou and Moussiades, 2020). Rule based chatbots are pattern matching, where there are pre-defined conversations written into the software and only the last input is taken into consideration, whereas Machine Learning uses NLP and considers the context of the entire conversation. The algorithm and tools are critical in these chatbot designs.

(Adamopoulou and Moussiades, 2020) If we were to further explore rule-based chatbot software, there are some available open-source examples at RiveScript and ChatScript. Rule based chatbots such as Clever Bot, Chatfuel, and Watson are just a few examples of pattern matching rule-based approaches to chatbot design (Ramesh, Ravishankaran, Joshi and Chandrasekaran, 2017).

Cleverbot affiliated with Google matches input with existing conversations to respond. The cleverscript manual was interesting as it details the process, they followed in populating the input and output of the bot. They listed key words with responses and then expanded on the phrases and the different variations of the input words to recognise a wide range of broad questions (Cleverscript manual, 2022). Chatfuel is an Instagram, Facebook chatbot used to increase sales and integrated with an internal CRM system. Watson is a chatbot used to provide a wide range of advice but is well-known for health advice and the accuracy Watson has in diagnosing conditions based on a large amount of data. (Watson Health Solutions, 2022).

I believe chatbots can improve services provided to staff to make their lives easier, and I would like to explore how we can develop a chatbot as a training tool by identifying vital information and planning the conversational experience.

Conversational UX (User eXperience)

Another element to consider is conversational user experience, I came across the Conversational User Experience Guide published in May 2021 included in Microsoft's Azure test bot on GitHub. It helps us to identify and plan further details of the chatbot development such as planning for the ideal conversational experience, personality, traits, ‘brand’, identifying key customer intents and implementing a strategy to work towards within the code. For replies we can also determine if the chatbot will use emojis, Gifs, how errors will be handled and how to end the conversation gracefully (Conversational User Experience Guide, 2021).

Benefits of the chatbot

Knowledge base

The chatbot could act as a purpose-built training tool for internal staff to assist them in answering queries that would not usually come up very often. Consistent answers are provided, and we can ensure the answers built into the code are in line with the Business values and quality check if needed.

Personalisation

The chatbot can be personalised to interact seamlessly according to the organisation's standards with staff, and it could resemble a human-to-human conversation.

Availability and Accessibility

Features 24-hour availability (Dilmegani, 2022) as it will be posted online in SharePoint. It is possible to process written input and provide audio responses with text-to-speech availability.

Instant responses

Instant responses (Dilmegani, 2022) will be provided online, and it can multitask if multiple people engage it. Due to the non-delay of responses, efficiency could increase work processing. (CHATBOTS ARE HERE TO STAY So what are you waiting for? 2017) Reduced costs as it does not require an annual employee salary with fewer resources required.

Sentiment Analysis

There are no emotional exchanges in times of stress as the chatbot will not respond to any angry responses. We could include training the chatbot to respond appropriately to de-escalate the situation if needed.

Self Service

In the year 2022, younger people are more likely to want to find the answer to their questions themselves online before asking a human. Shy or introverted personality types may prefer chatbot interactions to a human.

Updates, development, and review chatbot responses

FAQs (Frequently Asked Questions) and surveys can be reviewed within the organisation to provide regular updates to code. Feedback from staff interactions with the chatbot can be used to increase engagement. Recorded interactions can provide invaluable data to the organisation to understand roadblocks or pain points to staff that can be addressed. Over time we can expand chatbot understanding and services, making it more efficient.

  • NLTK

Outcome

If the project is successful, it would have a tool that could be used to answer questions instantly with natural conversation flow. Eventually, it would upskill staff or become a daily tool to provide repetitive answers to frequent questions and be trained over time to answer complex questions.

The original issue of training and support would be filled with this tool, and staff will be empowered to carry out their jobs knowing they can ask questions any time of the day and get an answer straight away. Productivity could improve, and morale in the teams would increase.
